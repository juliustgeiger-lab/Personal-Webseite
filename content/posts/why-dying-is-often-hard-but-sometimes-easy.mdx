## The doctor's office

You are sitting across from your doctor. You came in for a routine appointment that is supposed to end with "everything looks fine, see you next year." But it does not end that way. The doctor uses words you only half hear. *Terminal. Months, possibly. We can talk about what to do next.*

You will replay this moment for the rest of your life, however long that is. The fluorescent light over the desk. A child laughing somewhere down the hallway. The faint smell of disinfectant on your sleeve from the chair. A text message from a friend you forgot to answer this morning. None of it has changed in the last sixty seconds. You walk out of the office. The same faces sitting in the waiting room. The barista at the corner is still telling the same story. 

Nothing in the world has changed. Everything in your world has.



## Why is this so hard

Most people assume the difficulty of that moment is the discovery that you will die. It is not. You already knew you would die. You have known since you were five or six. The developmental research is fairly settled. The majority of healthy children in modern urban-industrial societies come to understand that death is irreversible, universal, final between the ages of five and seven [(Speece & Brent, 1984)](#ref-speece-brent-1984). By the time you can ride a bike, you have absorbed the fact that everything alive eventually stops. Death is not new information. You have been living with it for thirty years or fifty or seventy. 

The new information is the time horizon.

Until that appointment, you were operating with an implicit horizon of decades. Maybe you would not have written it down anywhere. You probably never made it explicit. But every decision you made was being weighed against that hidden number. The job you took because it would compound. The relationship you stayed in because you could imagine a long arc. The skills you were building. The savings you were accumulating. The trip you were going to take "next year." All of that planning rests on a horizon you never named but always assumed.

The doctor's visit just rewrote the number.



Two things have to happen at once. The first is grief. You have to mourn the futures that will not happen. The version of you who would have grown old, met grandchildren, finished the slow projects. Worse, you have to grieve the time you spent optimizing for a horizon that turned out to be wrong. Every sacrifice you made for the long term was made in a world that no longer exists. You delayed living to invest in a future that just got cancelled. The regret of that misallocation is its own kind of pain, separate from the fear of dying.

The second is recalibration. The horizon is now different. The strategy has to change. The decisions you make from this point forward have to be optimized for a new and much shorter timeframe. You cannot do this slowly. The whole point of having less time is that you have less time. Somewhere in the back of your mind a clock has started to tick louder.



## The structure underneath

The terminal illness example is the most extreme version of a problem that quietly governs every decision you make.

![Diagram showing the relationship between decision strategy and time horizon, with strategy informed by the expected horizon and influencing the actual horizon](/posts/horizon-diagram.jpg)

Every decision you make is calibrated against a time horizon, whether you have named that horizon or not. And the horizon is not one number. It is two.

The expected horizon is what you think the timeframe is. The actual horizon is what the timeframe turns out to be. They are almost never the same. People are also bad at estimating even the expected one [(Buehler, Griffin, & Ross, 1994)](#ref-buehler-1994), which means the input to every strategy is wrong before the strategy even runs.

Your expectation about how long the game lasts informs what strategy you choose. If you think you have forty years, you compound. If you think you have three, you concentrate. The expected horizon is the input. The strategy is the output.

But the strategy you pick does not just sit downstream of the horizon. It runs back the other way. The choices you make change how long the game actually lasts. A reckless strategy shortens it. A protective strategy extends it. Some strategies run a small chance of cutting the horizon to zero. Others bend it longer than you expected.

So the expected horizon shapes the strategy. The strategy shapes the actual horizon. The actual horizon is almost guaranteed to be different from the expected one, because it was the strategy that produced it. This is the "geometry" of every decision under uncertainty.

## How it plays out

Most of the time the structure is invisible because the gap between expected and actual is small enough that you do not notice. Sometimes the gap is enormous. The consequences are public.

A founder raises a Series A and quietly assumes the next round is eighteen months out. The strategy that follows is calibrated to that horizon. Hire ahead of revenue. Spend on growth. Push the gross margin question to next year. Then the market turns. The next round does not come. The runway that was supposed to last eighteen months runs out at fourteen. The strategy was correct for the expected horizon, although it got the company killed under the actual one.

The reverse plays out just as often. A retailer assumes the shift to e-commerce is decades away and keeps the long-arc strategy. Refurbish the stores. Deepen the supplier relationships. Train the floor staff. The expected horizon was thirty years of slow adaptation. The actual horizon was eight. The patient compounding work that would have paid off across thirty years became dead weight at year nine. Everything that was a strength inside the expected horizon turned into a liability under the actual one. [Christensen (1997)](#ref-christensen-1997) gave this its canonical form. Incumbents do not fail because they manage badly. They fail because they manage correctly for a horizon that disruption has already shortened.

You see it in careers. Someone bets a decade on a slow-compounding craft, expecting twenty more years of upside. The industry collapses in five. The compounding never gets to do its work. Or the opposite. Someone abandons a craft early because they expect a short window of relevance. They watch the people who stayed make a thirty-year arc out of the same starting position.

You see it in relationships. Someone stays in a relationship that is wrong for them because they are calibrating against forty more years of life. They tell themselves the right time to leave is later. Then either the relationship or the life ends earlier than they planned. The postponement turns out to have been the whole story.

In every one of these cases, the people involved optimized correctly for their expected horizon. So the failure point was not reasoning, but the inescapable structure of making decisions when the horizon is not knowable in advance.

There is no clean way out. You have to pick a strategy without knowing the horizon. The strategy you pick will partly determine the horizon you get. You will only learn what the actual horizon was after it is over.

## What if

The doctor's news lands hard when the life it interrupts was built on a single horizon assumption. Everything was calibrated for forty more years. The diagnosis does not just shorten the horizon. It exposes how much of the life was sitting inside the wrong frame. The grief is not really about dying. It is about the misallocation.

The same news lands differently when the life it interrupts was built barbell-shaped. Taleb's strategy of staying extremely safe with most of one's exposure and extremely aggressive with the rest, precisely so that no single horizon assumption breaks the whole portfolio [(Taleb, 2012)](#ref-taleb-2012). The barbell is, at root, a refusal to commit to one expected horizon. It buys survival under the short one and upside under the long one. It is one of the few moves available to you when you cannot know how long you have, because it does not require you to know. Some part of it is already running on a shorter horizon. The walks taken. The meals eaten with friends and family. The calls made. The risks taken in the work that mattered, regardless of payoff. Another part is running on the long horizon, but loosely, ready to be wrong. Nothing in such a life is fully bet on a number that the doctor could rewrite.

Dying is hard when you optimized for one horizon and got another. It is easier when you did not.

This is not a recipe for ease at the doctor's office. There is no recipe. Just a structural claim about why some lives feel finished even when they end early, while others, ended on schedule, feel cheated. The difference is not how long they ran. The difference is how much of them was committed to a horizon that nobody, ever, gets to know in advance.
