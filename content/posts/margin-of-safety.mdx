---
title: Margin of safety, beyond the balance sheet
date: 2026-04-15
excerpt: Graham's idea works for more than stocks.

The margin of safety is usually taught as a valuation rule: buy a dollar for
fifty cents, so you can be wrong and still survive. The mechanic is simple — the
thinking underneath it is not.

A margin of safety is an admission. It says: *I don't fully trust my inputs.*
It forces you to separate "what I think" from "what I'm betting on."

## Where it generalises

The same structure shows up everywhere you make a decision you can't easily
undo:

- **Hiring.** Hire the candidate you'd still want if your excitement faded
  twenty percent.
- **Commitments.** If a plan only works at the optimistic estimate, it doesn't
  work.
- **Deadlines.** Any schedule without slack is a schedule that breaks.

## The move, not the number

The number in a margin-of-safety calculation is almost a distraction. The move
is the *habit* of asking what has to be true for this to work, and then pricing
in the possibility that it isn't.

Do that honestly and you'll make fewer bold-sounding decisions. You'll also
survive more of them.
