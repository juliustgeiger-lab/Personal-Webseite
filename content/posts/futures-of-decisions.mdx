Pick a decision you're carrying. The job offer. The mortgage. The conversation you've been postponing. Now imagine the next decade played out a thousand times — each one a slightly different roll of the dice. In how many of those futures is your decision still good?

Most people don't think this way. They picture one future — usually the one their fear or hope happens to focus on — and weigh the decision against that. Either it goes well, or it doesn't.

Monte Carlo logic refuses to collapse the future into a single story. It insists on the cloud. A decision isn't a bet on the world being one specific way — it's a bet across the whole distribution of how the world might be.

Once you see that, the question changes. You stop asking *will this work out?* — there is no *this*, only thousands of variants — and start asking *in what fraction of futures does this work out, and in what fraction does it ruin me?*

The first number doesn't matter much. The second one does.

## The bimodal asymmetry

A decision can perform well in 70% of futures and still be a bad bet, if the 5% where it fails kill you.

A decision can perform poorly in 70% of futures and still be the right move, if the 30% where it works are quietly transformative.

That asymmetry is what most decision-making advice misses. It treats the average as the answer. But you don't live the average. You live one draw from the distribution — and the only one that ever happens to *you* is the one with the absorbing barrier in it, if there is one.

## What you can choose

You can't choose which future you'll land in. You don't get to pick. The world will play out exactly once, from your perspective, and the dice will fall how they fall.

What you can choose is the **shape of the bet** you're making across all of them. Whether the worst-case kills you or just stings. Whether the best-case is mediocre or transformative. Whether your downside is bounded or open-ended.

That's what good decisions optimise for, and it's almost never the most likely outcome. It's the structure of the distribution.

The honest answer to *will this work?* is: in a lot of futures yes, in some no, in a few catastrophically. The interesting question is which set of futures you can afford to land in — and whether the ones you can't are too big a slice to risk.
