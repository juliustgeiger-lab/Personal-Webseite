---
title: A good decision is not a good outcome
date: 2026-04-20
excerpt: Why process beats results when the world is noisy.
---

The clearest lesson from poker, investing, and most of life: a good decision and a
good outcome are separate things. You can decide well and lose. You can decide
badly and win. Over a short stretch, outcomes tell you almost nothing about
quality.

The reason is obvious once you say it out loud — the world injects noise between
decision and result. A single trial is a sample of one, drawn from a distribution
you don't fully see.

## What this means in practice

- **Judge the decision by what you knew at the time**, not by what happened after.
- **Expect to be wrong about outcomes** and right about process, or the reverse,
  more often than feels natural.
- **Keep a record.** A decision journal turns vague memory into comparable data.
  Without one, hindsight will rewrite everything.

## The uncomfortable corollary

If a good decision can lose, then a losing trade, a failed hire, or a bad
relationship don't automatically mean you decided badly. You still have to ask
the harder question: given what I knew, would I make the same call again?

That question is the entire game.
